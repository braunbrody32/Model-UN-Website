What We Do

About Model UN

Model United Nations (MUN) is an academic simulation of the United Nations where students step into the roles of delegates, representing countries and debating pressing global issues. It sharpens critical thinking, public speaking, negotiation, and cross-cultural understanding.

Our Mission

We aim to cultivate the next generation of global leaders by providing an immersive platform for diplomatic discourse. Through rigorous debate and collaborative resolution-drafting, delegates gain skills that transcend the conference room.

Why Participate?

Model UN builds essential life skills — persuasive communication, research depth, negotiation tactics, and the ability to find consensus across differing viewpoints. Whether you're a first-timer or a seasoned delegate, every conference leaves you sharper.

How It Works

Delegates are assigned countries and committees. They research their nation's position on topics, write position papers, and participate in formal debate sessions — including motions, amendments, and voting procedures — to produce working resolutions.

Our Committees

We host a range of committee types including General Assembly bodies, specialized agencies, and crisis committees — each with unique rules and procedures. Delegates from all experience levels will find a committee that challenges and excites them.

Procedure & Protocol

Rules of Procedure

Model UN follows Robert's Rules of Order adapted for UN-style debate. Understanding these procedures is essential for effective participation. Below are the core rules every delegate must know.

01

Roll Call & Quorum

Each session begins with a roll call. Delegates respond "Present" or "Present and Voting." A quorum (majority of enrolled delegates) must be established before debate can begin.

02

Setting the Agenda

Delegates vote to determine which topic will be addressed first. Any delegate may motion to set the agenda; a simple majority vote decides the order of topics for debate.

03

Speakers List

The formal speakers list governs general debate. Each delegate is given an equal speaking time (typically 60–90 seconds) to address the committee on the topic at hand.

04

Moderated Caucus

A delegate may motion for a moderated caucus, specifying a total time and speaking time. Delegates are recognized by the Chair to speak on a specific, narrowed subtopic.

05

Unmoderated Caucus

An unmoderated caucus is informal floor time used for lobbying, merging working papers, and building consensus. Delegates move freely and speak without formal recognition.

06

Working Papers

Working papers are pre-resolution documents that outline proposed solutions. They require a minimum number of signatories to be introduced and do not need to be approved by the Chair before circulation.

07

Draft Resolutions

A working paper that has been approved by the dais becomes a draft resolution. It is formatted with preambulatory and operative clauses and debated formally before a vote.

08

Amendments

Delegates may propose friendly (agreed upon by sponsors) or unfriendly amendments to a draft resolution. Unfriendly amendments require a procedural vote by the committee.

09

Voting Procedure

Voting is done by placard. Delegates who responded "Present and Voting" must vote Yes or No — abstentions are not allowed. Delegates who responded "Present" may abstain. A simple majority passes most resolutions.

10

Points of Order

A Point of Order is raised when a delegate believes proper procedure is not being followed. These interrupt debate and must be ruled on by the Chair before debate continues.

11

Points of Information

A Point of Information (or Parliamentary Inquiry) asks the Chair a procedural question. Delegates may also yield remaining speaking time to Points of Information from other delegates.

12

Delegate Conduct

All delegates must maintain decorum. Delegates speak in third-person ("The delegate of France…"), address the Chair respectfully, and refrain from personal attacks. The Chair may censure delegates for misconduct.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a Position Paper?

+

A position paper is a short document (typically 1–2 pages) that outlines your country's stance on the committee topic. It includes background on the issue from your country's perspective, past UN action, and proposed solutions. Position papers are usually submitted before the conference begins.

How do I make a motion?

+

Raise your placard and wait to be recognized by the Chair. Then clearly state your motion, for example: "The delegate of Germany motions for a 10-minute moderated caucus with a 60-second speaking time to discuss climate finance mechanisms." Motions require a second and a simple majority vote to pass.

What are operative clause starters?

+

Operative clauses begin each actionable paragraph of a resolution. Common starters include:

  • Calls upon
  • Urges
  • Encourages
  • Requests
  • Condemns
  • Decides
  • Recommends
  • Affirms

How do preambulatory clauses work?

+

Preambulatory clauses come before the operative section and establish context. They are italicized and begin with words like: Recalling, Reaffirming, Noting, Concerned by, Recognizing, Acknowledging, and Bearing in mind. They do not themselves enact anything — they set the stage for the operative clauses.

What happens during voting procedure?

+

When voting begins, no one may enter or leave the room. Delegates vote in alphabetical order by country. You may vote Yes, No, or Abstain (if you responded "Present" during roll call). After the vote, delegates may make Explanations of Vote to clarify their position on the record.

Tips for new delegates

+

Research your country thoroughly — know its foreign policy, alliances, and economic situation. Engage early: get on the speakers list in the first session. During unmoderated caucuses, approach other delegates to build alliances. Be flexible — great delegates compromise to build winning coalitions, not just to win arguments.
